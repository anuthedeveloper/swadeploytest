1. Anus

The final part of the digestive tract, through which feces are expelled from the body. It has two sphincters that control the expulsion of feces. The anus also plays a role in maintaining continence.

10. Mouth 2. Gallbladder
2. Gallbladder

The gallbladder stores and concentrates bile produced by the liver, releasing it into the small intestine to aid in fat digestion. Bile helps emulsify fats, making them easier to digest. The gallbladder releases bile in response to signals from the digestive system.

1. Anus 3. Salivary Glands
3. Salivary Glands

These glands produce saliva, which moistens food and begins the digestive process. Saliva contains amylase, an enzyme that starts breaking down starches into sugars. There are three pairs of salivary glands: the parotid, submandibular, and sublingual glands.

2. Gallbladder 4. Pancreas
4. Pancreas

The pancreas produces digestive enzymes and bicarbonate, which are released into the small intestine to aid digestion. It also has endocrine functions, releasing insulin and glucagon to regulate blood sugar levels. The enzymes from the pancreas help break down carbohydrates, proteins, and fats.

3. Salivary Glands 5. Esophagus (Gullet)
5. Esophagus (Gullet)

A muscular tube that transports food from the mouth to the stomach via peristalsis. Peristalsis is a series of wave-like muscle contractions that move food down the esophagus. The esophagus also has a sphincter that prevents acid reflux from the stomach.

4. Pancreas 6. Small intestine
6. Small intestine

The site of most digestion and nutrient absorption, consisting of the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. The small intestine is lined with villi and microvilli, which increase the surface area for absorption. Digestive enzymes and bile work together here to break down food into absorbable nutrients.

5. Esophagus (Gullet) 7. Liver
7. Liver

The liver produces bile, which helps digest fats and also processes nutrients absorbed from the small intestine. It detoxifies chemicals, metabolizes drugs, and synthesizes proteins important for blood clotting and other functions. The liver is essential for metabolic homeostasis.

6. Small intestine 8. Large intestine
8. Large intestine

The large intestine absorbs water and salts from the material that has not been digested as food and is responsible for the formation of feces. It includes the cecum, colon, rectum, and anal canal. The large intestine also houses beneficial bacteria that aid in fermentation of undigested materials.

7. Liver 9. Stomach
9. Stomach

The stomach is a sac-like organ where food is mixed with gastric juices to form chyme, initiating protein digestion. It secretes hydrochloric acid and enzymes that digest proteins. The stomach’s churning action further breaks down food particles.

8. Large intestine 10. Mouth
10. Mouth

The entry point for food, where mechanical digestion begins with chewing, and chemical digestion starts with saliva. Saliva contains enzymes that break down carbohydrates. The mouth also plays a role in taste and speech.
