1. Nucleoid (DNA)

The region in the cell where the circular DNA molecule is located. Unlike eukaryotic cells, prokaryotes do not have a membrane-bound nucleus. The nucleoid contains the genetic information necessary for cell function and reproduction.

2. Ribosomes

The structures responsible for protein synthesis. Prokaryotic ribosomes are smaller than eukaryotic ribosomes and are the target for certain antibiotics. They translate genetic information from the nucleoid into proteins.

3. Cell Wall

A rigid layer made of peptidoglycan that provides structural support and shape to the cell. The cell wall protects the cell from osmotic pressure changes. It is a crucial target for many antibiotics.

2. Ribosomes 4. Capsule
4. Capsule

A gelatinous layer surrounding the cell wall that protects the cell from desiccation and phagocytosis. The capsule enhances the ability of bacteria to cause disease (pathogenicity). It also helps in adhering to surfaces and evading the immune system.

3. Cell Wall 5. Cytoplasm
5. Cytoplasm

The gel-like substance inside the cell where metabolic reactions occur. The cytoplasm contains enzymes, nutrients, waste products, and other cell components. It is the site of many biochemical pathways.

4. Capsule 6. Plasma Membrane
6. Plasma Membrane

A phospholipid bilayer that controls the movement of substances into and out of the cell. The plasma membrane is involved in various cellular processes, including respiration and nutrient transport. It contains proteins that function in signaling and maintaining the cell’s internal environment.

5. Cytoplasm 7. Pilus
7. Pilus

Hair-like structures on the surface of the cell that help in attachment to surfaces and in the exchange of genetic material during conjugation. Pili are important for bacterial colonization and biofilm formation. They play a role in pathogenicity by allowing bacteria to adhere to host tissues.
